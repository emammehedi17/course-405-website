1. What is Research?

  • "Scientific research is a systematic, controlled, empirical, and critical investigation of natural phenomena guided by theory and hypotheses about the presumed relations among such phenomena." (Kerlinger, F. N. (1986). Foundations of Behavioral Research).
  • "Research is a process of steps used to collect and analyze information to increase our understanding of a topic or issue." (Creswell, J. W. (2014). Research Design: Qualitative, Quantitative, and Mixed Methods Approaches).

Research is a systematic and rigorous process of inquiry that involves collecting and analyzing evidence to discover new knowledge, test or revise existing theories, and answer questions in a credible and verifiable way. It moves beyond common sense by employing a structured methodology to ensure that conclusions are based on solid evidence rather than mere intuition or flawed perception.

2. Why Research is Necessary

Research is essential because it produces evidence-based facts, knowledge, and ideas that go beyond individual experience. It is a systematic process that allows us to understand the world with greater accuracy and confidence. The necessity of research stems from several key reasons:

  • It is based on the collective experiences of many people, often using a representative sample to ensure findings are generalizable.
  • It requires verifiable data and evidence to support its conclusions.
  • It follows a rigorous and structured methodology, ensuring the process is transparent and repeatable.
  • Its findings can be used for prediction and to forecast future trends or outcomes.
  • It provides a reliable foundation for policy formulation in various sectors, including government, health, and business.

Common Sense vs. Research

Basic Categories of Knowledge

Scott (2002) identifies six fundamental categories of knowledge that shape our understanding of the world:

  1. Common-sense knowledge: Refers to things that are widely known and accepted as true within a society, such as the knowledge that fire burns.
  2. Authority-based knowledge: Knowledge acquired from expert sources that are given credibility, such as doctors, scientists, or other professionals.
  3. Experiential knowledge: Knowledge developed from an individual's own personal experiences, which can sometimes conflict with expert or authoritative knowledge.
  4. Traditional knowledge: Beliefs and practices passed down through generations that explain and justify various aspects of life.
  5. Non-rational knowledge: Knowledge based on faith, intuition, or divine revelation, such as a belief in God.
  6. Scientific knowledge: Knowledge that is generated through a process of systematic, rigorous testing and empirical observation.

3. When a Problem is Researchable?

For a problem to be considered researchable, three conditions should be met:

  1. There is a perceived discrepancy between what currently is and what should be.
  2. A question exists as to why this discrepancy is present.
  3. There are at least two possible and plausible answers to that question.

4. What is Social Research?

Social Research is a scientific undertaking which, by means of logical and systematized techniques, aims to:

  • Discover new facts or verify and test old facts.
  • Analyze their sequence, interrelationships, and causal explanations, which were derived within an appropriate theoretical frame of reference.
  • Develop new scientific tools, concepts, and theories that facilitate the reliable and valid study of human behavior.

(Source: P.V. Young, Scientific Social Survey and Research)

5. Objectives of Research:

The primary purpose of research is to discover answers through the application of scientific procedures. The main objectives are:

  • Descriptive Research: To portray accurately the characteristics of a particular individual, situation, or group.
  • Exploratory Research: To gain familiarity with a phenomenon or to achieve new insights into it.
  • Explanatory Research: To determine the frequency with which something occurs or with which it is associated with something else.
  • Hypothesis-Testing Research: To test a hypothesis of a causal relationship between variables.

6. Characteristics of Research:

Effective research has several key characteristics:

  • It is directed towards the solution of a specific problem.
  • It is based upon observable experience or empirical evidence.
  • It demands accurate observation and description.
  • It involves gathering new data from primary sources or using existing data for a new purpose.
  • Its activities are characterized by carefully designed procedures.
  • It requires expertise and skill in investigation, literature search, and data analysis.
  • It is objective and logical, applying every possible test to validate the data and conclusions.
  • It involves a quest for answers to unsolved problems.
  • It requires courage, patience, and unhurried activity.
  • It is carefully recorded and reported.

7. The Scientific Method

What is Science?

The word 'Science' originates from the Latin 'Scire', meaning 'To Know'. It is a body of systematic and organized knowledge acquired through the scientific method. Science is distinct because of its methodology, which involves observing phenomena, establishing relationships between them, and formulating theories.

Purpose of Science

The purpose of science is to observe and understand the world. This involves three major components:

  1. Description: Describing what is observed without relating it to other things.
  2. Discovering Regularities/Patterns: Finding patterns in phenomena and establishing relationships.
  3. Formulation of Theories and Laws: Drawing conclusions about the salient features of the subject of study.

The Scientific Method

The scientific method is the systematic collection of data (facts) and their theoretical treatment through proper observation, experimentation, and interpretation. It aims to achieve a systematic interrelation of facts.

Basic Assumptions in the Scientific Method

  • It relies on empirical evidence.
  • It utilizes relevant concepts.
  • It is committed to objective considerations.
  • It presupposes ethical neutrality.
  • It results in probabilistic predictions.
  • The methodology is made known to others.
  • It aims at formulating scientific theories.

Characteristics of Scientific Activities

  • Logical: It assumes causal relationships and logical consequences (Inductive and Deductive).
  • Deterministic: It assumes every action has a cause or antecedent event.
  • General: It aims for general understanding rather than explaining individual events.
  • Parsimonious: It seeks to explain the most with the fewest variables ("Less variables, More Explanation").
  • Specific: The process of measurement must be specific and clean.
  • Empirically Verifiable: Conclusions are based on physical, verifiable evidence.
  • Inter-Subjective: It creates a clear, objective reality that scientists can agree upon despite different backgrounds.
  • Open to Modification: It is not static and allows for change, modification, and falsification.

8. Social Science

Social science deals with human behavior and its socio-economic and cultural aspects. While it is considered a science, it faces unique challenges, such as difficulties in measuring social phenomena, issues with respondent accuracy, and discovering social regularities.

Characteristics of Social Science

  • Logical: Generally applicable, but human behavior is not always rational.
  • Deterministic: Fully applicable; every social action or event has a cause.
  • General: Partly applicable (e.g., marriage is universal, but its forms vary).
  • Parsimonious: Applicable to some extent, particularly in fields like economics.
  • Specific: Highly applicable; definitions, units, and variables must be explicit.
  • Empirically Verifiable: Completely and entirely applicable.
  • Inter-Subjective: Not fully applicable, as researcher bias (value judgments) can be difficult to eliminate.
  • Open to Modification: Social science theories can be rebuilt and are not static.

Objectives of Social Science

The ultimate goal of social science is to produce an accumulating body of reliable knowledge. Its aims are:

  1. Explanation: To discover why events happen and explain the reasons behind them.
  2. Prediction: To make predictions based on experience and understanding of antecedents.
  3. Understanding: To apply scientific characteristics to explain human behavior, acknowledging the unique complexities involved.

9. Criteria of a Good Research

  • The purpose is clearly defined.
  • The research process is detailed.
  • The research design is thoroughly planned.
  • High ethical standards are applied.
  • Limitations are frankly revealed.
  • Analysis is adequate for the decision-maker's needs.
  • Findings are presented unambiguously.
  • Conclusions are justified.
  • The researcher's experience is reflected.

10. Qualities of a Good Research

  • Systematic: Good research follows a structured and orderly process. This means it is not haphazard but proceeds through a logical sequence of steps, from defining a problem and reviewing literature to collecting and analyzing data, and finally drawing conclusions. This ensures the research is thorough and well-organized.
  • Logical: The entire research process is based on valid procedures and principles of reasoning. Both inductive and deductive reasoning are used to ensure that the arguments are sound and the conclusions logically follow from the evidence.
  • Empirical: Research is rooted in the real world. It is based on objective evidence collected through direct or indirect observation and experience, rather than on abstract theory or belief alone.
  • Replicable: The procedures and methods used in the research are described in sufficient detail so that another researcher can repeat the study under similar conditions. If the results are similar, it increases confidence in their validity and reliability.
  • Creative: Good research is not just about following a formula. It can involve thinking of new ways to approach a problem, developing innovative methods for collecting data, or interpreting findings in a novel and insightful way.
  • Use of multiple methods: A problem can often be investigated more thoroughly by combining different research techniques (a practice known as triangulation). Using multiple methods can provide a more comprehensive and robust understanding of the research problem.

11. Need for Research

  • Exploration: To explore areas where little is known.
    Example: A researcher might conduct an exploratory study on the social dynamics of a newly formed online community.
  • Description: To describe individuals, situations, or groups accurately.
    Example: A census is a descriptive study that aims to accurately describe the demographic characteristics of a country's population.
  • Diagnosis: To diagnose the frequency of a problem or its association with other factors.
    Example: A health researcher might conduct a study to determine the prevalence of diabetes in a specific urban area.
  • Hypothesis Testing: To test causal relationships between variables.
    Example: An educational psychologist might test the hypothesis that a new teaching method causes an improvement in student test scores.
  • Inductions and Deductions: To develop general theories from specific observations (induction) or test specific hypotheses from general theories (deduction).
    Example (Induction): From observing that educated people often live near green spaces, a sociologist might induce a theory about a positive relationship between education and access to such spaces.

12. Scope of Research

Research is significant across numerous domains:

  • Decision Making: It informs decision-making, highlights risks, and identifies alternative courses of action.
  • Resource Management: It helps in the economic use of resources and in project identification.
  • Business and Industry: It solves problems related to investment, pricing, allocation, HR, operations, and planning.
  • Government: It provides the basis for government policies.
  • Social Science: It helps social scientists study social relationships and find answers to social problems.
  • Personal and Professional Growth: It serves as a career path for students, a source of livelihood for professionals, an outlet for new ideas for thinkers, a means for creative development for literary figures, and a way to generalize new theories for analysts.

13. Types of Research

Research Types According to Objectives:

  1. Descriptive Research: A fact-finding investigation aimed at describing the characteristics of an individual, situation, or group as it exists.
  2. Exploratory Research: A preliminary study of an unfamiliar problem about which the researcher has little knowledge.
  3. Explanatory Research: Aims to explain the underlying reasons for, causes of, and relationships between variables.
  4. Experimental Research: Designed to assess the effect of one variable on another by keeping other variables constant or controlled.

On the Basis of Outcome:

  • Fundamental (or Basic/Pure) Research: Undertaken for the sake of knowledge without any intention to apply it in practice.
  • Applied (or Action) Research: Carried out to find a solution to a real-life problem requiring an action or policy decision.

On the Basis of Inquiry Mode:

  • Quantitative Research: Is employed for measuring the quantity or amount of a particular phenomenon by the use of statistical analysis.
  • Qualitative Research: Is a non-quantitative type of analysis which is aimed at finding out the quality of a particular phenomenon.
  • Mixed Methods Research: Involves collecting, analyzing, and integrating both quantitative and qualitative data in a single study.

On the Basis of Concept:

  • Conceptual Research: Is generally used by philosophers and thinkers to develop new concepts or to reinterpret existing ones.
  • Empirical Research: Is a data-based research which depends on experience or observation alone.

14. The Research-Theory Cycle

Research and theory exist in a cyclical relationship.

  1. Theory: A theory is used to formulate a testable hypothesis.
  2. Hypothesis: A study is designed to test the hypothesis.
  3. Research (Observation): The research is performed, and data is gathered.
  4. Modification: The results are used to create or modify the theory, starting the cycle over again.

15. The Research Process

The research process consists of a series of sequential steps. Let's use an example research problem: Investigating the relationship between social media use and sleep quality among university students.

  1. Define Research Problem: Identifying and formulating the problem.
  2. Review the Literature: Surveying existing concepts, theories, and previous research findings.
  3. Formulate Hypothesis: Developing a testable proposition.
  4. Design Research: Creating a plan for the study, including sample design.
  5. Collect Data: Executing the research plan and gathering data.
  6. Analyze Data: Testing the hypothesis, if any.
  7. Interpret and Report: Drawing conclusions and presenting the findings.

16. Step 1: Defining the Research Problem

What is a Research Problem?

A research problem refers to a difficulty or need that a researcher experiences in a theoretical or practical situation and for which they want to find a solution.

Sources of Research Problems

  • Reading
  • Academic and Daily Experiences
  • Exposure to Field Situations
  • Consultations and Brainstorming
  • Previous Research
  • Intuition

17. Step 2: Literature Review

Definition of Literature Review

A literature review is the documentation of a comprehensive survey of published and unpublished work from secondary sources in an area of specific interest to the researcher.

Purpose of Literature Review

  • To gain background knowledge of the research topic.
  • To identify key concepts, the potential relationships between them, and to formulate a researchable hypothesis.
  • To identify appropriate methodology, research design, methods of measuring concepts, and techniques of analysis.
  • To identify data sources used by other researchers.
  • To learn how others have structured their reports and findings.

18. Research Design

Research Design refers to the plan, structure, and strategy of research—it is the blueprint that will guide the entire research process.

Process of Designing a Research Project

This process outlines the key questions and tasks at each stage of the research project, from introduction and literature review to methodology and conclusions.

Research Design vs. Research Methods

  • Research Design: The overall plan to answer your research question. It is the strategic framework.
  • Research Method: The specific strategy or tool used to implement the plan (e.g., a survey, an experiment).

19. Sampling Techniques & Size

Sampling is the process of selecting a smaller, representative collection of units from a population to determine truths about that population.

Importance of Sampling

  • Efficiency (saves time and money).
  • Accuracy.
  • Feasibility.

Important Statistical Terms

  • Population: The complete set of items of interest.
  • Sample: A subset of the population.
  • Target Population: The specific population for the conclusion.
  • Sampling Unit: The basic unit to be sampled.
  • Sampling Frame: A list of all sampling units.
  • Sampling Scheme: The method of selecting units.

20. Types of Sampling

Non-Probability Sampling

Selection is not random; it relies on the subjective judgment of the researcher. Types include Convenience, Purposive, Quota, and Snowball sampling.

Probability Sampling

Every unit has a known, non-zero chance of being selected. This allows for generalization. Types include:

  • Simple Random Sampling (SRS)
  • Systematic Sampling
  • Stratified Sampling
  • Cluster Sampling
  • Multistage Sampling
